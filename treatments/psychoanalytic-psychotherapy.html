Psychoanalytic Psychotherapy

Psychoanalytic psychotherapy originates from psychoanalysis and Sigmund Freud, but has evolved into several different schools of thought.

Psychoanalytic psychotherapy mainly works with a person's unconscious processes and the way these affect our mental state,feelings and behaviour. It focuses on transference, in lay terms, the way one relates to one's therapist while also exploring a person's everyday life and relationships. It usually is a longer form of therapy where issues are explored in great depth and detail emphasizing how our early relationships shape our current life attitude and how we can work our way to change this. The past is revisited time and again only in the light of the here-and-now. Change does not only come from therapeutic understanding but also from relating and the revisiting of our core self.

Psychoanalytic Psychotherapy is the most widely known form of therapy and often the most misconstrued through the film and the media. It is a form of therapy that can be very demanding and challenging but equally rewarding as it aims in internal restructuring of who we are. It requires commitment on the part of the patient and rigorous work. The therapist and patient typically meet two to three times or more a week but is not uncustomary for a once a week pace in some cases. The therapy times and days remain relatively constant during the therapeutic period.
